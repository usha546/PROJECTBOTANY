Phloem transport
 

Translocation of organic solutes in plants refers to the movement of sugars, amino acids, and other organic
 compounds throughout the plant body, primarily through the phloem. This transport occurs from areas where 
 these solutes are produced or stored (sources) to areas where they are needed for growth, development, or
  storage (sinks). 
Here's a more detailed explanation:
1. The Role of Phloem:
The phloem is a specialized vascular tissue that conducts these organic solutes. 
It consists of sieve tubes, which are long chains of living cells that allow for the movement of sap
(the phloem's fluid). 
2. Sources and Sinks:
Sources:
These are areas where organic compounds are synthesized or stored, such as leaves (during photosynthesis) or
 storage organs like roots or stems. 
Sinks:
These are areas where organic compounds are needed for growth, development, or storage, such as developing 
fruits, roots, or young leaves. 
3. Direction of Transport:
Translocation can occur in both directions (bidirectional). 
Downward translocation is the most common, moving food from leaves to other parts of the plant. 
Upward translocation also occurs, moving nutrients from the roots to the shoots. 
 



Mass flow hypothesis


The mass flow hypothesis, also known as the pressure-flow hypothesis, explains how sap, particularly sugars,travels
 through the phloem in plants. It proposes that sap moves from areas of high sugar concentration (sources, like leaves)
 to areas of low sugar concentration (sinks, like roots or fruits) due to pressure
differences. This movement is driven by the flow of water and dissolved solutes, essentially a 
"pressure-driven" flow. 
How it works:
1. Sugar Loading:
In source cells (like leaf cells), sugars (like glucose) are actively transported into the phloem sieve tubes increasing 
 the solute concentration there. 
2. Water Entry:
This high solute concentration in the phloem creates a water potential gradient, drawing water from the adjacent
xylem (water-conducting tissue) and companion cells into the phloem via osmosis. 
3. Increased Pressure:
The influx of water increases the turgor pressure (hydrostatic pressure) within the sieve tube elements in the source. 
4. Flow to Sink:
This pressure difference drives the mass flow of sap (containing sugars) from the source to the sink,
 where sugars are unloaded for utilization. 
5. Water Exit:
At the sink, sugars are removed from the phloem, decreasing the solute concentration and water potential.
 Water is then drawn out of the phloem and back into the xylem.
